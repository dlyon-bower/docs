---
title: "Personal context"
description: "Tell Bird about you once — your role, expertise, and preferences — and every conversation starts calibrated."
---

Bird is helpful out of the box, but every conversation starts cold. It doesn't know whether you're a PI or a first-year grad student, whether you want concise answers or detailed ones, or whether you prefer AMA citations over Vancouver. Personal context fixes that.

**Personal context** is a short note you write *to Bird, about you*. It's injected into every conversation Bird has with you, so you don't have to keep re-introducing yourself or restating preferences. Visible only to you — never to your workspace.

## Why use it

Without personal context, you end up repeating the same setup every conversation:

> "I'm a postdoc, you can skip the basics. Use AMA citations. Keep it concise."

With personal context, you write that once in settings and Bird reads it before every reply.

The benefits compound across daily use:

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Calibrated explanations">
    Bird tailors depth and vocabulary to your expertise. Tell it you're an expert in mass spectrometry but new to single-cell RNA-seq, and it will explain the latter without dumbing down the former.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Consistent communication style">
    Want concise responses? Specific citation style? A preferred tone? State it once. Bird applies it across chat, voice, and the live agent.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Hard preferences that stick">
    Things that previously needed correcting in every conversation — "spell my name with the accent", "never use em-dashes", "always include a Methods bullet" — get respected from turn one.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Faster onboarding for new conversations">
    No more "first turn = configure Bird, second turn = actual question". Every chat opens already focused on what you need.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## How to write it

Free text. No format required. Write to Bird like you'd brief a new lab partner.

A useful starting structure:

- **Who you are** — role, field, experience level
- **Where Bird should calibrate up or down** — topics you know cold, topics you're still learning
- **How you like to communicate** — response length, tone, citation style
- **Hard preferences** — anything Bird has gotten wrong before, anything non-negotiable

### Example

```
I'm a postdoc in computational neuroscience, four years in. Comfortable with
single-cell methods, Python, and statistical modelling. Still learning systems
neuroscience and electrophysiology — explain those topics like I'm new.

Communication:
- Default to concise. Bullet points where it helps; prose where it doesn't.
- AMA citation style. No footnotes.
- No em-dashes.

Currently preparing an R01 renewal due 2026-06-01.
```

## Where to set it

**Settings → Preferences → Personal context for Bird**

Type or paste your context, hit **Save**. Changes apply to new conversations — Bird reads memory at the start of each conversation, so existing chats keep their original context until they're closed and reopened.

The 6 KB cap is plenty for personal preferences. If you find yourself near the limit, prune older items rather than expanding — Bird performs best with focused, current context.

## Privacy

<Note>
  Personal context is **strictly private to you**. No one in your workspace, including admins and owners, can read or edit it. It's stored encrypted in your account and only injected into Bird conversations where you are the requester.
</Note>

If you delete your account, your personal context is deleted with it.

## Personal vs. workspace memory

Bird has two memory layers, and they compose:

|  | Personal context | [Workspace memory](/ai-and-search/workspace-memory) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Visible to | Only you | All workspace members |
| Edited by | Only you | Owners and admins |
| Use it for | Your role, preferences, hard rules | Lab vocabulary, conventions, standing instructions |
| Conflicts | Workspace rules win on compliance; personal preferences win on style | — |

Use personal context for *you*. Use workspace memory for *the lab*.

## Tips

<Tip>
  **Keep it current.** Update your personal context when your focus changes — a new project, a deadline, a topic you're suddenly going deep on. Bird is more useful when its context reflects your present work, not your past.
</Tip>

<Tip>
  **Lead with the most surprising thing.** If there's one preference Bird often misses (e.g. "I always want SI units, not imperial"), put it near the top.
</Tip>

<Tip>
  **Use plain language.** No need to format as a list, write headers, or use markdown. Bird understands natural prose.
</Tip>
